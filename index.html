THE RULE OF
RECIPROCALITY

Generally, countries base their visa policies on the principle of reciprocity. This means that country A is very likely to treat the citizens of country B the way country B treats the citizens of country A in terms of visa policies.

So, to a large extent, how China treats other citizens in visa policies also affects the level of difficulty for the Chinese to travel abroad.

THE NOTORIOUS

HUKOU SYSTEM

(AKA THE RESIDENCE REGISTRATION SYSTEM)

The Hukou System creates many problems, and one of it is making obtaining a passport very difficult.

How many trips and how much “additional information” does it take before you can have a passport?

  1. Miss certificate of non-criminal clearance.
  2. Miss certificate of employment.
  3. Miss the business license of the company.
  4. Miss proof of qualifications for the business trips abroad.
  5. Not have a local ID card.
  6. Finally have the passport.

Zhou’s
Story

When it comes to the issue of passport procurement, the hukou system manifests its problems. For a person who has moved away from the place where his hukou originally is, this person has to return to his/her origin to obtain passport, because everything has to be done in person at the place where his/her hukou is.
       On October 11th, 2013, Focus Talk (“Jiao Dian Fang Tan”) on China Central Television (CCTV) reported a story of Mr. Zhou, who came from Wuyi, Hubei Province and was currently working in Beijing. Last year, his company planned to send him on a business trip abroad.
       For the half year before his trip, he returned to his hometown from Beijing 5 times to submit “additional materials”, which were not listed as must-have materials but were required by the Entry and Exit Administration in Wuyi. After the case exposed by the media, he finally got his passport on the 6th trip.
       Zhou's story is definitely not a stand-alone case.

*Even though Zhou's residence is in Wuyi, where he is born, and he works in Beijing, he got his ID card in the province of Inner Mongolia, when he was studying there. Therefore, he does not have a local ID card of Wuyi, Hebei Province.

CHINA'S STRICT BORDER CONTROL: CHINESE

NOT-SO-EASY GO

Even if a Chinese doesn’t need a visa or can have a visa upon arrival for certian countries, he/she might not be able to do that because Chinese airlines and customs require a visa for the destination regardless.

Although airlines, unlike Exit and Entry Administration, do not possess the real power to forbid someone to leave the country, they usually serve as the first line of defense before the real custom inspection.
       In the summer of 2013, I was traveling from Chengdu, China to Rabat, Morocco. Traveling on a low budget, I chose to stop three times along the way. Since I booked at different companies, I was told that I had to retrieve my luggage after I arrived at Dubai. I knew that Dubai had a policy of allowing travelers with an outbound ticket within 96 hours of arrival to travel within United Arab Emirates (UAE) on a 96-hour transit visa, which traveler could obtain upon arrival. I was very sure of it because I had done this one time before in the past and also I looked it up online before my travel that it still applied to me as a Chinese citizen.
       However, the airline told me that it would not allow me to do that because I did not have a visa to enter UAE to retrieve my luggage in my passport at the moment. The person working at the check-in had to call her supervisor to confirm what they should do in this situation. Fortunately or unfortunately, the flight was cancelled due to weather issue, and I had to fly on a different route that did not involve leaving the airport to retrieve my luggage so it worked or else, there was a good chance that I would have to give up my tickets because the airline did not allow people to leave the country without a visa even when a visa was not necessary.
I understand that the problem with the airline above might not apply to all airports and airlines across China, but the issue with customs control can serve as the last line of border control. For instance, Iran is one of the few countries that allow Chinese citizens to obtain a visa upon arrival for a short stay. However, according numerous online discussions that I found in China’s travel forums (e.g. tuniu.com and douban.com) many customs inspections across China forbid Chinese citizens to go to Iran without a visa, even though they have purchased the flight tickets and they can obtain a visa upon arrival.
       People who discussed this online suggested some ways to get around this. As a Chinese citizens, in order to obtain the visa upon arrival for Iran, you can either go to Hong Kong or Macau first (where nobody cares about people leaving the border and where the customs inspection is left to the country of arrival) and then fly from Hong Kong to Iran, or go to another country first and then go from that country to Iran. Either way, it would be a hassle, because you are still forced to obtain another permit, either the special passport for Hong Kong/Macau only or the visa to visit another country.

Say, you want an American B (Travel/Business) Visa, these are things you want to prepare for sure...

  • A nonimmigrant Visa Electronic Application (DS-160);
  • The passport of the applicant;
  • One 2” × 2” photo taken within the last six months;
  • Potentially “an additional visa issuance reciprocity fee, depending on your nationality”, which “respond[s] to the total of similar visa fees charged to US citizens by a particular country”;
  • Application fee of $160 for B Visa;
  • Reservation at a designated call center;
  • Other supporting documents, such as:
    • Current proof of income, tax payments, property or business ownership, or assets;
    • Travel itinerary and/or other explanation about your planned trip;
    • A letter from your employer detailing your position, salary, how long you have been employed, and authorized vacation, and the business purpose, if any, of your U.S. trip;
    • Criminal/court records pertaining to any arrest or conviction anywhere, even if you completed your sentence or were later pardoned;
    • Documents attesting to your previous visits to the United States;
    • Depending on different occupations, the applicants should also provide additional documents proving their identities.
